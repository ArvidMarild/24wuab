"Face Eater" by Dana Schutz is a provocative and enigmatic painting that confronts viewers with its unsettling imagery. In this work, Schutz employs bold colors and dynamic brushwork to depict a figure devouring its own face. The composition is both chaotic and mesmerizing, drawing the viewer into a realm where the boundaries between the real and the surreal blur. Through "Face Eater," Schutz explores themes of identity, consumption, and the complexity of the human psyche, inviting viewers to contemplate the darker aspects of existence.

This exhibition invites you to explore the world of Edvard Munch —his ideas, processes, and the profoundly human topics that occupied him and that still affect us today. Through a wide selection from the museum’s collection, you can experience the richness of Munch’s artistic career and his unrelenting drive to experiment and innovate. The exhibition provides the opportunity to engage with themes and motifs that Munch explored his whole life in the form of paintings, graphics, drawing, photography, and sculpture—the tales of anxiety, death, love, and loneliness, which we all have in common.

The Gothenburg Colourists were a group of Swedish artists active in the early 20th century, primarily during the first three decades. Influenced by the French Impressionists and Post-Impressionists, particularly Paul Cézanne, as well as the Neo-Impressionists, the Gothenburg Colourists developed a unique style characterized by vibrant color palettes and bold brushwork. The group included prominent artists such as Nils Kreuger, Ragnar Sandberg, and Ivan Aguéli, among others.
